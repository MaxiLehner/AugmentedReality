The first AR system

The first realisation this technology started with.

Shortly thereafter, Sutherland constructed the first VR system. In 1968, he finished the first head-mounted display.
Because of its weight, it had to be suspended from the ceiling and was appropriately nicknamed “Sword of Damocles”.
This display already included head tracking and used see-through optics.

Advancing

People still keep trying to advance the technology to this day.

Advances in computing performance of the 1980s and early 1990s were ultimately required for AR to emerge as an independent field of research.
Throughout the 1970s and 1980s, Myron Krueger, Dan Sandin, Scott Fisher, and others had experimented with many concepts of mixing human interaction
with computer-generated overlays on video for interactive art experiences.
Krueger [1991], in particular, demonstrated collaborative interactive overlays of graphical annotations among participant silhouettes in his Videoplace installations around 1974.

Naming the technology

People needed an exact term for the technology, so it can spread much faster and that they can remember the name.

The year 1992 marked the birth of the term “augmented reality.”
This term first appeared in the work of Caudell and Mizell [1992] at Boeing, which sought to assist workers in an airplane factory by displaying
wire bundle assembly schematics in a see-through HMD.

Latest steps

After a long time of advancing the technology and letting the name of it spread, companies managed to make profit out of it.

The term augmented reality became better known to the mass market in spring 2012, when Google introduced Google Glasses, data glasses that did not meet
all the criteria of the previously presented AR technology, but also added virtual information to reality. However, these glasses could not prevail,
so the project was postponed in 2015. Nevertheless, Google stayed on the topic and in October 2014 invested an additional 500 million US dollars in the company Magic Leap,
which, according to its own statements, is developing revolutionary AR glasses. Microsoft is already one step further and has already brought
the first AR glasses onto the market with Microsoft Hololens.

In the field of handheld AR systems, Nintendo, in cooperation with Niantic, triggered a real hype with the Pokémon Go smartphone app in summer 2016.
Within the first three months, the game, in which virtual characters can be caught in the real environment of the user, generated sales of over 600 million US dollars.
